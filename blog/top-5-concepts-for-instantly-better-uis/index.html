Prologue

Many of us, web developers, throw a big chunk of our attention to the technical aspect of web development, we learn frameworks as soon as they come out, we follow out-of-the-oven news for browser support, we anticipate new language specifications as they get dished out to us. I am not proclaiming that we shrug off the aesthetic aspect, because we actually do give it a lot of care as well, it is often crazy, we follow latest typography trends, latest color palette trends, latest photography trends, even latest whether-to-use-gradients-or-flat-colors trends. Now, before this turns into a rant I want to proclaim that I am not against any of this; This is all good; however, these are extras, I’ve witnessed many people blindly follow these so-called “design trends” that I personally have nothing against, unless it is for the critical expense of overlooking the core, eccentric principles of design, then we have a serious issue.

I found this one example that illustrates my argument perfectly, I am sure it will get - slightly? - controversial when I tell you that the example I am about to show you is a winner of the prestigious SOTD Awwwards award, which is something every web developer looks up to, have a look:

] Open link

After the first-glance’s charm wears off and as you scroll down the page you will start noticing how difficult it is to read the text, in fact, its exhausting to do so. First of all, there is very little contrast between the light gray text and the white-ish background, also the background is fighting for attention with the text in a very annoying manner thus rendering the page inefficient in delivering its message since its very painful to read, of course there is no doubt that design wise this site is superb, also it is very creative as per the colorful circles that characterize the theme of the website, and we can see this reflected in its Awwwards scores, It got a 7.25 and 7.00 in Design and Creativity respectively; on the other hand, it got a score of a 6.75 in Usability, according to Wikipedia, usability is defined as:

… the capacity of a system to provide a condition for its users to perform the tasks safely, effectively, and efficiently while enjoying the experience.

Now we can see how this definition correlate to what we’ve witnessed in the website, the website or rather the design was incapable of delivering its message effectively and efficiently, some sharp-sighted dude or dudette out there might be saying: “But.. but I was able to see read the text clearly, I got the site’s message, what you are saying is BS!”, so for this dude/dudette I say: number don’t lie, I took color samples from some background-foreground pairs in the website, ran them through https://contrast-ratio.com/ and ended up with a contrast ratio ranging from 1.79 to 4.91 where everything below 4.50 is deemed bad according to WCAG 2.0 (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines v2.0), don’t take my word for it, you got the site link, take your color picker, head to contrast-ratio.com and see for yourself how most of the typography on the site fails the contrast test or at least fails for small text, which is pretty redundant in that site.

Bottom line is, if the aforementioned site omitted fancy effects which is clearly something that is time & effort inefficient, and focused on just the core principals of UI design, the result would’ve attained much more clarity, produced much more impact and delivered its message better.

A wise man once said:

A pyramid’s capstone is necessary for perfection; however, placing the capstone before the core will get you nowhere close to it.
